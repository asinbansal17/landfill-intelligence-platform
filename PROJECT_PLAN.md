### Week 3: Sweden & Japan Research (7 hours)

#### Task 3.1: Sweden Deep-Dive (3.5 hours)

Research and document:

**1. Current State**
- Recycling rate: 99%
- Landfills: 1-2 (40+ year capacity)
- Daily waste per capita: 1 kg/person/day
- Where it goes: 99% recycled/composted, 1% incinerated

**2. How They Achieved It**
- Timeline: 1975-2020 (3 phases)
  - Phase 1 (1975-1995): Build infrastructure
  - Phase 2 (1995-2010): Extended Producer Responsibility
  - Phase 3 (2010-2020): Zero waste to landfill
- Key policies: Ban on organic waste, EPR system, waste tax
- Budget: Started with $1-2B/year, now $5B/year
- Enforcement: Strong government + legal penalties

**3. Economic Impact**
- Jobs in recycling: 50,000+
- Revenue from recycled materials: $800M/year
- Cost savings (less pollution): $1.2B/year

**4. Key Success Factors**
- Strong government enforcement
- Economic incentives (not just punishment)
- Public trust (75%+ trust government)
- Wealthy nation (can afford investment)
- 45 years of consistent policy

**5. Why India Can't Copy Yet**
- Investment gap: Sweden $500/person vs India $2/person (250x)
- Enforcement: Sweden high, India low (corruption)
- Trust: Sweden 75%, India 35% (public trust in government)
- Culture: Different public discipline levels

**Sources:**
- Swedish EPA (Naturvårdsverket) official reports
- World Bank waste data
- Academic papers
- News articles (last 5 years)

**Output:** 3-page Sweden detailed report

### Week 5: India National Situation (7 hours)

#### Task 5.1: Current Landfill Situation (3.5 hours)

Research and document:

**1. Landfill Crisis**
- Total landfills: 3,100+ across India
- Daily waste: 377,000 tons
- Recycling rate: 5%
- Where waste goes:
  - 75% landfill/dumping
  - 10% burned (air pollution)
  - 10% rivers/streets
  - 5% recycled

**2. Most Affected Cities**
- Delhi: 14,000 tons/day, 2-3 years capacity left 🔴
- Mumbai: 10,000 tons/day, 4-5 years capacity left 🔴
- Bangalore: 6,000 tons/day, 3-4 years capacity left 🔴
- Kolkata: 4,500 tons/day, 2 years capacity left 🔴
- Chennai, Hyderabad, Pune: Similar crisis

**3. Health & Environmental Impact**
- Air pollution: Methane from landfills = 10% of India's emissions
- Water pollution: Leachate contaminates groundwater
- Health: 1.2M deaths/year from air pollution (WHO estimate)
- Economic cost: $120B/year (health + cleanup)

**4. Current Government Response**
- Law: Solid Waste Management Rules 2016 (good law, poor enforcement)
- Ministry: Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change
- Implementation: Weak at state/municipal level
- Budget: $280M/year (for 1.4B people) = $2/person/year

**Sources:**
- CPCB annual reports
- Ministry of Environment India
- World Bank South Asia waste reports
- News articles about landfill crises
- NGO reports (Chintan, ICES)

**Output:** 2-page India National Crisis report

#### Task 5.2: Root Causes Analysis (3.5 hours)

Research WHY India's system fails:

**1. Budget Constraint**
- India spends: $280M/year = $2/person/year
- Sweden spends: $5B/year = $500/person/year
- Gap: 250x less than Sweden
- Reality: India's GDP/capita = $2,500 (vs Sweden's $60,000)
- Can India afford it? Yes, with political will: $20-50/person/year possible

**2. Weak Enforcement**
- Law exists but not enforced
- Only 100 inspectors for 3,100 landfills
- Corruption: Officials bribed to ignore violations
- Low penalties: Fine is $100 but violation cost is $1M
- Result: No consequences for illegal dumping

**3. Infrastructure Gap**
- Recycling centers: Only 500 for 1.4B people
- Needed: 50,000+ facilities
- Composting: Minimal infrastructure
- Waste segregation: Only 20% of waste is sorted

**4. Public Behavior**
- Waste segregation: 80% of Indians don't separate waste
- Why: Not taught, no consequences, no incentives
- Littering: Widespread despite being illegal
- Result: Clean waste management impossible

**5. Corruption & Politics**
- Landfill contracts: Given to political allies, not capability
- Illegal dumping: Allowed for bribes
- Reform: Blocked by vested interests

**6. Limited Private Sector**
- Recycling companies: Few incentives to operate
- Government doesn't pay for recycling
- No market for recycled materials
- Result: Almost no private waste management sector

**Sources:**
- Government audit reports (CAG)
- NGO investigations
- News articles about corruption
- Academic papers on India waste
- International reports on enforcement

**Output:** 2-page Root Causes & Barriers report
